Breadcrumb Navigation

A redesigned breadcrumb experience for clarity, hierarchy, and efficiency
At Mercedes-Benz R&D India, I led the redesign of the breadcrumb navigation system for our internal dashboard application. The goal was to improve navigational clarity, provide better context within complex dashboard hierarchies, and reduce cognitive load across screen sizes.

In large dashboards, breadcrumbs play a critical role in navigation. By redesigning how navigation history is shown, we enhanced user orientation and made complex workflows easier to follow.
Background

The existing breadcrumb solution in our application only showed the origin and current page with a dropdown in between. This was limiting in scenarios where users navigated deeply into workflows or multi-page setups, resulting in confusion and backtracking issues.

Target

To create a responsive breadcrumb component that:

  • Reflects real navigation paths
  • Adapts to screen sizes
  • Provides quick access to up to four visible steps
  • Clearly distinguishes the current location with styling
  • Includes a dropdown for extended history

Research & Discovery

Identifying inconsistencies and aligning teams through audits, interviews, and industry benchmarking.

1. Mapped the full navigation hierarchy across the application

To understand where breadcrumbs were missing or misaligned, I began by mapping the entire application’s navigation hierarchy. This included all modules, pages, and nested workflows, especially those with multiple entry points or frequent back-and-forth movement.

Key issues found
  • Breadcrumbs didn’t reflect the actual journey taken
  • Only origin and current page shown, with unclear middle history
  • No visual hierarchy to distinguish levels
  • Unclickable intermediate steps reduced efficiency
  • Dropdown too limited and non-intuitive on mobile
2. Silent Observation (Germany Users)

Conducted screen recordings and passive observation sessions with users from the German office. Without interrupting, I watched how users navigated complex flows, which paths they retraced often, and where they expected breadcrumbs to support them.

Key findings
  • Users frequently relied on browser back buttons when breadcrumbs failed.
  • The most common return points were always the Dashboard and Overview pages.
3. User Interviews

Held 6 contextual interviews with product users to understand frustrations, expectations from breadcrumb navigation, and how they typically "go back" during their tasks. This revealed patterns around frequently accessed screens and missing cues in the current navigation.

Key findings
  • Users preferred always seeing their start point and the most recent 1–2 visited pages.
  • There was a clear desire for clickable breadcrumb trails, not just labels.
4. Stakeholder Conversations

Regular syncs with PMs and tech leads clarified which screens (like Dashboard and Overview) were non-negotiables for visibility in breadcrumbs. These insights helped define the logic behind which pages should always remain visible—even when condensing the breadcrumb trail.

Key findings
  • Older pages beyond the third step could be safely tucked into a dropdown.
  • Visibility of primary navigational anchors (Dashboard, Overview) increased user trust.

What happens when users can’t retrace their steps or understand where they are in a complex workflow?

Users often found it difficult to orient themselves within multi-layered modules. The lack of clear breadcrumb navigation led to confusion, repetitive backtracking, and errors. A contextual, well-structured breadcrumb system was essential to improve clarity and reduce friction.

What do we want to achieve?
  • Provide users with a clear, always-visible path of where they are.
  • Allow quick navigation back to key sections like dashboards or overviews.
  • Handle long breadcrumb trails gracefully without overwhelming the UI.
How do we want to achieve it?

  • Study real usage patterns and map out navigation hierarchies across modules.
  • Prioritize frequently accessed sections (like Dashboard & Overview) as always-visible in the breadcrumb trail.
  • Introduce dynamic ellipsis logic: when more than 4 items exist, collapse the older pages into a dropdown, starting from the fourth item.
  • Design responsive breadcrumb behavior across screen sizes (desktop, tablet, mobile).
  • Document each component with usage rules, behaviors, and accessibility notes
  • Validate the new breadcrumb behavior with users via interviews, observations, and stakeholder discussions.

What I Built

Identifying inconsistencies and aligning teams through audits, interviews, and industry benchmarking.

Landing Page Intentionally Omitted

User behavior analysis revealed that the landing page is only accessed once during the initial session. Since users never return to it during task flows, we excluded it from the breadcrumb trail to reduce clutter and improve relevance.

Visual Hierarchy for Dashboards

To help users quickly identify key sections, dashboards are displayed in bold within the breadcrumb trail. This typographic distinction improves scannability and reinforces their importance within the navigation path.

Clarity in Breadcrumb Display Rules

Overview pages are shown in regular text to maintain a balanced visual hierarchy. External applications, being outside the core flow, are excluded from the breadcrumb trail to avoid unnecessary complexity and keep navigation focused.

Structured Naming Format

Level 2 pages in the breadcrumb follow a consistent naming convention that includes a unique identifier followed by a secondary reference. This predictable structure makes it easy for users to recognize detailed views within complex workflows. Therefore, no additional visual identifiers were needed in the breadcrumb itself to convey page type or context.

Breadcrumb Logic & Rules
Breadcrumb Expansion & Click Behavior
  • Breadcrumbs grow dynamically as the user navigates deeper into the product.
  • All items are clickable except for the last item, which represents the current page.
Maximum Length & Ellipsis Dropdown
  • Only five breadcrumb levels are displayed at a time.
  • When the path exceeds five pages, an ellipsis (...) appears at the third position, indicating a dropdown.
  • The first item (starting point) and the last item (current page) are always visible.
  • The dropdown can hold up to 10 earlier pages, preserving navigation history.
Visual Hierarchy & Recognition
  • Different font styles (e.g., bold for dashboards, regular for overview pages) indicate the page level/type.
  • This visual structure helps users quickly understand where they are and where they came from.

Final Outcome

1. Improved Navigation Efficiency

Users were able to retrace steps faster, especially in multi-level workflows, reducing time spent navigating between sections.

2. User Confidence & Orientation

The improved visual hierarchy and contextual depth (through font styles and structured paths) helped users feel more grounded and aware of where they are.

3. Positive Feedback from Target Users

Internal users in Germany reported that the new breadcrumb experience felt “clearer,” “more predictable,” and easier to use during testing and rollout.
